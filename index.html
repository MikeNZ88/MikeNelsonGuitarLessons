About Mike

I am an experienced guitarist with over 25 years of playing experience. I have taught students young and old, from complete beginners to intermediate level players who need to improve their technique or get out of a rut!

My Approach to Lessons

Lessons usually focus on a specific song chosen by the student—or by me if it is useful to develop specific skills. Technique and theory are discussed in the context of the song. There are also dedicated lessons solely for technique and theory. The type of lesson and content depends on your goals and the style of playing you wish to develop.

Song Lessons

  • Quick Check-In: A brief chat to see how your song practice is going and identify any challenges.
  • Song: We work on a specific section of your song where a key skill (like alternate picking or chord transitions) needs improvement. This helps with learning the song and developing your technical skills.
  • Musical Ideas in Context: You'll learn how the musical ideas behind the song connect to underlying theory.
  • Immediate Feedback: Targeted, hands-on corrections help you make quick progress.
  • Practice Instructions: Dedicate focused time to practicing the song section, applying the techniques we worked on, and reviewing any theory concepts discussed.

Theory-Focused Lessons

  • Music Theory Concepts: These sessions explore scales, chord structures, rhythmic concepts, and common forms (e.g., 12 bar blues) to build a solid foundation for your musical intuition.
  • Application: Learn how to apply these concepts in your playing through songwriting and improvisation.

Technique-Focused Lessons

  • These sessions concentrate on refining your technical skills through targeted exercises and drills. These may be new techniques or ones you are developing.

This comprehensive approach ensures that every lesson—whether focused on a song, theory, or technique—contributes to your overall development as a guitarist.

Curriculum Overview

Each level is organized under the following categories:

  • Chord Fundamentals
  • Melodic/Single Note Playing
  • Rhythmic Development
  • Music Theory
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills
  • Technique

Beginner (Foundations)

Key Skills:

  • Chord Fundamentals: Master basic open chords such as A, Am, C, D, Dm, E, Em, and G, focusing on smooth, clean transitions.
  • Melodic/Single Note Playing: Practice simple melodies on one or two strings to build finger strength and accuracy.
  • Rhythmic Development: Use simple strumming patterns (quarter notes and basic eighth notes) while tapping your foot to maintain a steady internal pulse.
  • Music Theory: Learn note names and understand how the third interval differentiates major from minor chords.
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills: Practice identifying note names along the E string using targeted exercises and basic fretboard diagrams; develop the ability to interpret chord charts and lyric sheets.
  • Technique: Master an effective, comfortable pick grip and focus on correct hand placement.

Advanced Beginner (Building Blocks)

Key Skills:

  • Chord Fundamentals: Continue working with basic chords and introduce additional open chords and open 7th chords (e.g., A7, D7) with wider transitions.
  • Melodic/Single Note Playing: Practice and refine single‑note melodies; begin integrating simple multi‑string riffs.
  • Rhythmic Development: Explore varied strumming patterns that incorporate subtle syncopation.
  • Music Theory: Expand your understanding of chord construction using chord charts and fretboard diagrams; begin exploring foundational forms like the 12‑bar blues.
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills: Reinforce note identification along the E string, extend familiarity to other strings, and enhance skills in reading chord and lyric sheets.
  • Technique: Continue to master an effective, comfortable pick grip and proper hand placement.

Intermediate (Progressing)

Key Skills:

  • Chord Fundamentals: Introduce and practice barre chords; refine overall chord transitions.
  • Melodic/Single Note Playing: Develop scale proficiency using single‑note patterns (covering the Pentatonic, major, and relative minor scales) to build speed, accuracy, and vocabulary.
  • Rhythmic Development: Enhance chord transitions with varied rhythmic patterns (e.g., sixteenth notes, triplets) and practice with a metronome for precise timing.
  • Music Theory: Study intervals and diatonic harmony using practical, tab‑based examples and written explanations.
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills: Develop proficiency in reading chord, lyric, and tablature sheets.
  • Technique: Focus on executing hammer‑ons, pull‑offs, slides, palm muting, and incorporate ghost notes for added rhythmic and tonal variation.

Advanced Intermediate (Refining Skills)

Key Skills:

  • Chord Fundamentals: Continue refining complex chord transitions with increased speed and precision.
  • Melodic/Single Note Playing: Expand your use of scales, modes, and arpeggio exercises.
  • Rhythmic Development: Integrate complex rhythmic patterns (e.g., sixteenth notes, triplets, syncopation) for enhanced expressive timing.
  • Music Theory: Deepen your understanding of modal concepts and chord substitutions through practical examples and detailed written materials.
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills: Maintain proficiency in reading advanced tablature and chord charts.
  • Technique: Master advanced techniques such as tapping, string muting, alternate picking, and pinch harmonics.

Advanced (Mastery)

Key Skills:

  • Chord Mastery: Master advanced chord shapes, including extended and altered chords.
  • Scale Mastery: Explore advanced scales (diminished, whole tone, harmonic minor, melodic minor, and their modes) and integrate them into solos and improvisation.
  • Musical Expression: Develop nuanced performance with dynamic variation, precise alternate picking, and expressive phrasing.
  • Advanced Rhythmic Development: Practice intricate subdivisions (complex sixteenth note passages, triplets, dotted rhythms) within sophisticated arrangements.
  • Advanced Music Theory: Study harmonic analysis, modulation, voice leading, and advanced chord substitutions using comprehensive written materials and diagrams.
  • Fretboard & Reading Skills: Continue advanced reading of complex tablature and chord charts.
  • Technique: Incorporate advanced picking techniques such as sweep picking and pick slanting for fluid, expressive lead playing.

Barre Chords

Barre chords are formed by using the index finger as a movable capo, allowing you to shift open chord shapes up and down the neck. The A-shape and E-shape barre chords are especially versatile, enabling you to play major, minor, suspended chords, and the three most common types of 7th chords.
